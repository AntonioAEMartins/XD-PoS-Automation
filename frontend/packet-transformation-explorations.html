Packet transformation · explorations

Six ways to read 482 bytes.

The current section on the landing page is a chip with three stages stacked top-to-bottom. These are six explorations of the same idea — the chip is gone, the layout is horizontal, and the language is more like "we noticed a pattern" and less like "TLV-encoded payload". Same fixture throughout: one pre-bill, table 12, captured once.

Approach A · slider

The scrubber.

One handle, three stops. 1 · what we sniffed → 2 · what it really was → 3 · what it actually meant. The crunch in the middle is honest — bytes literally collapsing into named fields — and then the third step drops the technicality and just tells you what the box was being asked to do.

One pre-bill, on the wire. 482 bytes, no structure visible to the naked eye.

Drag the handle. Or click any of the three labels.

Approach B · translation pane

Source & target.

The wire is always on the left. On the right, you choose the reading: as a message, as JSON, or as the action it actually was. Like a translation app, except the source language is "bytes nobody documented".

Source · the wire 482 B · TCP · port 8099
Target · how we read it

Same bytes, grouped at the [NP] separators. Each field has a [EQ] in the middle, like key=value.

The QUEUE field is Base64. Decoded, it's just JSON — and the JSON is the thing the box actually runs.

      Approach E · drag the lens
      
Run a lens across the wire.

      

        One long byte stream, one draggable lens. Whatever the lens is
        sitting on, it shows you what that region actually meant. No three
        stages — just one continuous read, like running your finger along
        a line of foreign script.
      

    


    

      

        

          

            Lens is on
            POSTQUEUE
          

          

            The opcode. "Hey box, I'm posting something to your queue."
            Always the very first nine bytes of any message of this kind.
          

        


        

          

          

          

        

        
Drag the glassy rectangle — it snaps to whatever zone of the wire it’s closest to.

      

    

  


  
  

    

      Approach F · filmstrip
      
Three frames, scrolling sideways.

      

        Read it like a silent film. Each frame is a "title card" with a
        short caption and a single artifact. Scroll horizontally, or use
        the dots / arrows. The animation between frames is the scroll
        itself — nothing magical, no morphs.
